Project study

collaborative museum studies program

organized by the Nanjing Foreign Language School and the Da Bao en
Temple Site Museum
I participated in a collaborative museum studies program jointly
organized by the Nanjing Foreign Language School and the Da Bao en
Temple Site Museum. The program integrated cultural heritage studies
with material science and chemistry, offering an in-depth exploration of
how modern scientific methods can be applied to the preservation,
analysis, and restoration of ancient artifacts and architectural
remains. It was divided into three main components: on-site
archaeological fieldwork, curatorial and materials study, and an
independent research project.

During the fieldwork module, I visited the Zhang Zhao Tomb and the ruins
of the ancient Changgan City. Through these visits, I was introduced to
various scientific techniques used in archaeological investigation, such
as radiocarbon dating (C-14 analysis) for chronological determination
and spectroscopic methods (including infrared and UV visible
spectroscopy) for analyzing material composition. I was particularly
fascinated by how chemistry provides a bridge between the tangible
relics of the past and our scientific understanding of time and process.
For example, learning about the role of isotopic decay in carbon dating
gave me a new appreciation of how fundamental chemical
principles half-life, isotopic ratios, and radioactive equilibrium can
literally measure time in the archaeological record.

The second part of the program focused on the Da Bao en Temple Site
Museum itself, where we studied both the artifacts and the unique
architectural techniques employed in the temple’s reconstruction and
preservation. We explored the ancient Chinese art of glazed tile and
colored glass production, investigating how materials science explains
the distinctive properties of these historical materials. From a
chemical standpoint, I learned that ancient liuli (glazed glass) differs
from modern glass primarily in composition and microstructure. Ancient
liuli typically contains high lead oxide and low silica content, which
enhances its refractive index and color brilliance but also increases
its fragility and susceptibility to corrosion.

We also analyzed the chemical processes involved in the production of
colored glazes, such as the use of metal oxides (e.g., Cu²⁺ for
green-blue, Fe³⁺ for amber tones, and Co²⁺ for deep blue). Understanding
the redox reactions that influence color formation helped me see how
chemistry not only enables the creation of beauty but also governs its
preservation. Moreover, by studying the differences between glass and
liuli in terms of ionic mobility, crystallinity, and resistance to
weathering, I gained a better sense of why some ancient artifacts retain
their brilliance over millennia while others deteriorate rapidly.

In the laboratory component, we learned about the chemical conservation
and restoration of artifacts. I had the opportunity to synthesize and
decompose basic copper carbonate (Cu₂(OH)₂CO₃), a key compound in
patinas and a product of copper corrosion, to understand both
degradation mechanisms and potential restoration approaches. By
comparing synthetic and naturally weathered samples under controlled
conditions, I could observe how pH, humidity, and temperature influence
the stability of carbonate layers. These experiments deepened my
understanding of how chemical equilibrium and solubility product (Ksp)
play critical roles in the long-term preservation of metal-based
artifacts.

The final stage of the program was a self-directed research project,
where I chose to investigate the application of glass–ceramic composite
materials in the restoration of liuli architectural elements. My
research aimed to explore how modern material synthesis techniques could
address the limitations of traditional liuli, such as fragility and poor
weather resistance, while preserving its aesthetic and historical
authenticity. I examined the controlled crystallization process in
glass–ceramics, focusing on the role of nucleating agents (like TiO₂ and
ZrO₂) in promoting uniform crystal growth within a glass matrix. This
balance between amorphous and crystalline phases offers improved
mechanical strength and durability—properties essential for outdoor
architectural restoration.

To support this study, I reviewed both historical literature and recent
research on solid-state reactions, ion diffusion, and
microstructure-property relationships in glass–ceramic systems. I also
compared thermal and optical properties of different silicate-based
composites, analyzing how factors like melting temperature, refractive
index, and expansion coefficient could be optimized to achieve
compatibility with ancient liuli. This independent project not only
enhanced my ability to connect chemical principles to real-world
cultural heritage applications but also trained me in basic academic
research methods—structuring a hypothesis, reviewing literature, and
organizing technical information into a coherent scientific argument.

Beyond the scientific knowledge, the program gave me a profound sense of
the interdisciplinary power of chemistry. It demonstrated how molecular
understanding can inform fields as diverse as art history, archaeology,
and architecture. Standing in front of centuries-old relics, I realized
that preserving them is not only an act of cultural respect but also a
chemical challenge—a test of how well we understand materials,
reactions, and the passage of time.

Overall, this museum program helped me integrate my interests in
chemistry and cultural heritage. It strengthened my scientific
foundation in material chemistry while cultivating a more humanistic
perspective on science as a means of preservation rather than
exploitation. The experience convinced me that the future of heritage
conservation lies in collaboration between scientists and historians,
and it inspired me to continue exploring how chemical innovation can
protect and revitalize the legacy of the past.

Competition

UK Chemistry Olympiad

From late 2024 to January 24th, 2025, I dedicated myself to preparing
for the UK Chemistry Olympiad (UKCHO). This period was one of the most
intellectually demanding yet rewarding experiences in my study of
chemistry. The preparation process not only deepened my understanding of
organic and inorganic principles but also honed my ability to think
logically, creatively, and independently.

Through constant practice, I developed a strong ability to identify the
characteristics of functional groups and predict reaction mechanisms
using logic and pattern recognition. I learned to recognize recurring
structural motifs, electron flow tendencies, and reaction intermediates.
Rather than memorizing every transformation, I focused on understanding
the reasoning behind them—how molecular structure determines reactivity
and how electronic effects guide reaction pathways. This approach
allowed me to derive reaction outcomes through deduction rather than
recall. I believe this ability to reason through unfamiliar situations
and intuitively predict chemical behavior will be essential in my future
studies and research, especially when dealing with unknown compounds or
designing new synthetic routes.

During my preparation, I often encountered past paper questions
involving unfamiliar substances or reactions, sometimes even containing
functional groups I had never learned before. Initially, this was
frustrating, as the Olympiad’s level far exceeded standard curriculum
content. However, over time I realized that each unfamiliar question was
an opportunity to test my conceptual flexibility. By analyzing known
functional groups and recalling reaction patterns from previous
problems, I learned to use analogy and chemical reasoning to predict
plausible outcomes. For instance, when presented with an unknown
compound, I would first identify its electron-withdrawing or
electron-donating substituents, infer its likely reactivity, and then
relate it to similar mechanisms I had studied earlier. This analytical
process trained my ability to extrapolate from first principles—a skill
central to advanced chemical research.

Beyond problem-solving, preparing for the UKCHO also improved my exam
strategy and discipline. Each past paper required not only technical
knowledge but also time management and precision in calculation. I
learned to organize my work systematically, balance qualitative
reasoning with quantitative accuracy, and communicate my logic clearly
through chemical equations and mechanisms. These habits have
strengthened my scientific mindset: careful, structured, and driven by
understanding rather than memorization.

Most importantly, this preparation experience changed how I perceive
chemistry as a discipline. I came to appreciate chemistry not as a
collection of isolated reactions, but as a coherent system governed by
logical principles and energetic constraints. It is a language of
patterns—where recognizing underlying relationships allows one to
navigate even the most complex problems.

Harvard Undergraduate Auscr Summit for Young Leaders in China (HSYLC)

In August 2024, I participated in the Harvard Undergraduate Auscr Summit for Young Leaders in China (HSYLC), where I enrolled in three elective courses. Among them, the most memorable was “Regression Curve Prediction,” a course focused on the fundamentals of statistical analysis. I learned how to apply regression models to identify relationships between variables, use Excel to organize and calculate correlation data, and visualize results through regression curve plotting. I chose this course because I believed that statistical tools are highly applicable to analyzing experimental data in chemistry research.

Beyond the classroom, I also participated in the Capstone Project, where my group presented our findings through a poster and oral presentation.This experience taught me how to communicate complex data clearly and collaborate effectively in an academic environment.

Through HSYLC, I not only strengthened my understanding of mathematics,
statistics, and chemistry, but also developed a more interdisciplinary
perspective. It inspired me to integrate quantitative reasoning into
scientific inquiry and deepened my interest in combining data analysis
with experimental science.

Project study

UCL Online Summer School

In July 2024, I attended the University College London (UCL) Online Summer School, which focused on the theme of reducing carbon emissionsand mitigating global warming. The program was highly interdisciplinary, combining elements of chemistry, environmental science, and engineering to explore how scientific innovation can address climate change.

Among the many lectures and discussions, the sessions that impressed me most were those examining the chemical properties of greenhouse gases such as carbon dioxide (CO₂) and methane (CH₄), and how modifications in chemical processes can help reduce their emissions. For example, I learned how improving catalytic efficiency and developing sustainable industrial reactions can lower CO₂ output in energy production and manufacturing. These topics deepened my understanding of the chemical foundations of climate change and showed me how theoretical chemistry can be transformed into practical environmental solutions.

One of the most valuable aspects of the program was my first experience with academic research and scientific writing. Our final assignment required us to produce a review paper on global warming, which introduced me to literature searching, citation practices, and academic structure. I learned how to use keywords effectively to locate relevant journal articles, evaluate their reliability, and summarize findings systematically. Although it was challenging at first, this process helped me develop critical reading and writing skills essential for future scientific research.

The summer school also emphasized the importance of collaboration and communication in science. Through group discussions and online presentations, I had the opportunity to exchange ideas with students from different academic and cultural backgrounds. These interactions broadened my perspective and helped me understand how global cooperation is essential in addressing environmental challenges. I also gained confidence in expressing my ideas clearly in English, especially when discussing complex scientific concepts.

Through this program, I developed not only academic knowledge but also the mindset of a researcher—curious, analytical, and determined to apply science for the greater good. The experience reaffirmed my aspiration to study and work at the intersection of chemistry and environmental innovation, where I can contribute to creating a cleaner, more sustainable world.

2024 British Biology Olympiad

From January to April 2024, I dedicated myself to preparing for the British Biology Olympiad (BBO), culminating in the examination on April 6th. During these four months of intensive study, I not only deepened my understanding of biological concepts but also developed a new way of thinking—approaching the world through the logical and systematic lens of biology.

The most valuable lesson I learned from preparing for the BBO was how biology provides a rational and evidence-based framework for understanding the world. Through studying topics such as natural selection, enzyme kinetics, and population dynamics, I realized that biological phenomena are not random, but governed by patterns that can be observed, tested, and explained. For instance, learning about feedback regulation in homeostasis helped me see how living organisms maintain internal balance through precise molecular control, just as ecosystems maintain stability through interdependent relationships. Similarly, studying Mendelian genetics and evolutionary mechanisms allowed me to appreciate how diversity in life results from small, predictable changes accumulating over time. This logical and structured way of thinking—identifying mechanisms, analyzing variables, and tracing causality—has influenced how I view not just biology, but the entire
natural world.

Another important aspect of my preparation involved integrating knowledge from chemistry to better understand biological processes. When reviewing molecular biology and biochemistry sections, I found that concepts like DNA structure, protein folding, and enzyme catalysis all rely heavily on chemical principles. For example, understanding how the hydrophilic and hydrophobic properties of amino acids affect protein structure helped me connect molecular interactions with larger biological functions. Likewise, learning about hydrogen bonding and base-pairing in DNA revealed how chemistry governs the storage and transmission of genetic information. These insights made me realize that biology and chemistry are deeply interconnected—biology describes what happens in living systems, while chemistry explains how it happens.

Beyond academic knowledge, preparing for the BBO trained my persistence and analytical thinking. The process demanded extensive reading, problem-solving, and the ability to synthesize information from different branches of biology—ecology, physiology, biochemistry, and genetics. There were times when the complexity felt overwhelming, but every challenge strengthened my curiosity and confidence in scientific inquiry. I learned to approach each question not by memorizing facts, but by reasoning through mechanisms and applying cross-disciplinary logic.

For the finial result, I got global silver. It was a little bit upset, but I would inspire myself to prepare for BBO more carefully and wisely, in order to get process in the next-year’s exam.

Conference

Oxford Model United Nations Conference

From April 4th to 6th, 2024, I participated in OXMUN, the Oxford Model
United Nations conference held in Shanghai. I represented India in the
UN Women committee, where our discussions centered on violence against
women in workplaces and public spaces. Although I did not receive an
award, this experience was deeply meaningful and provided me with
valuable insights into international dialogue, gender issues, and
personal growth.

One of the first things I noticed at the conference was the diversity
and sophistication of the delegates. Many participants came from
international schools in Shanghai, and compared to them, I immediately
felt the difference in background and exposure. They spoke English
fluently and naturally, were well-versed in international affairs, and
had access to global learning opportunities such as exchange programs
and political science electives. As a student from a second-tier city, I
initially felt self-conscious about my comparatively limited experience
and less polished communication.

However, this feeling gradually transformed into self-awareness and
confidence. Through conversations with other delegates, I realized that
while I might lack certain advantages in language or global exposure, I
possessed other strengths that were equally important—creativity,
practical reasoning, and sensitivity to local realities. These qualities
became especially valuable during our debates, where we discussed how to
combat gender-based violence in developing regions.

Drawing on my understanding of social and economic disparities, I
proposed an initiative to establish women-centered companies in
underdeveloped areas, built upon local industries and resources. Such
companies would serve two key purposes: first, to help women achieve
economic independence by providing stable employment, and second, to
educate women about their rights and self-protection. This approach
focused not just on external aid but on creating sustainable,
community-driven change. My proposal sparked enthusiastic discussion
among delegates and was eventually incorporated into the final
resolution—a moment that filled me with pride and reaffirmed the value
of thinking from multiple perspectives.

Beyond policy discussions, OXMUN also taught me how to find my place in
a highly competitive environment. I learned that diplomacy is not only
about eloquence or confidence, but also about observation, empathy, and
the courage to share ideas that matter. Instead of comparing myself to
others, I began to appreciate what I could contribute—a grounded
understanding of feasibility and innovation rooted in real-world
context.

In the end, OXMUN 2024 became an experience of self-discovery. It
broadened my global awareness, strengthened my communication skills, and
encouraged me to view challenges as opportunities to learn rather than
limitations. More importantly, it reminded me that meaningful
participation in global dialogue comes not from where one begins, but
from how one thinks, learns, and acts to make a difference.

Competition

35th National Youth Scienceand Technology Innovation Competition

In the March of 2024, I participated in the 35th National Youth Science and Technology Innovation Competition with my research project titled
“Promoting Low-Carbon Campuses through a Water-Source Heat Pump District Cooling and Heating System under the Dual-Carbon Strategy.” This experience marked one of the most meaningful stages in my academic journey, as it allowed me to apply scientific thinking to real-world sustainability issues while developing technical, analytical, and communication skills.

The core of my research was to explore how new energy systems could
replace traditional air-conditioning refrigerants such as Freon, which
are major contributors to greenhouse gas emissions. By investigating the
potential of a water-source heat pump system that utilizes river water
as a thermal reservoir, I examined how renewable thermal energy could be
integrated into campus-scale cooling and heating. To support my
analysis, I used OpenStudio, an energy modeling software, to
simulate the system’s performance and estimate the potential reduction
in carbon emissions when applied to a school environment. Through these
simulations, I learned to quantify how sustainable technologies can
contribute to China’s dual-carbon goals—carbon peaking and carbon
neutrality.

Throughout the research process, I gained valuable experience in using
modeling tools and data-driven approaches to evaluate energy systems.
Before this project, I had limited knowledge of energy modeling, but
learning to operate OpenStudio taught me how to translate real-world
conditions into computational models. I discovered the importance of
setting appropriate parameters, validating assumptions, and interpreting
data critically. More importantly, I realized that scientific innovation
is not only about generating new ideas but also about supporting them
with clear evidence and quantitative analysis.

Another significant part of the competition was the final defense
session. Presenting my research to the judges and experts helped me
improve my ability to communicate complex scientific ideas in a clear
and accessible way. I learned to organize my arguments logically,
highlight key findings effectively, and respond to questions with
confidence. This process taught me how to bridge the gap between
technical research and public understanding—a skill essential for future
scientists and innovators.

Ultimately, my project was awarded the First Prize at the Jiangsu
Provincial Level, which was both an honor and a motivation for me to
continue exploring sustainable technologies. Beyond the recognition, the
experience reinforced my belief in the importance of interdisciplinary
learning. By combining principles from environmental science,
thermodynamics, and data modeling, I discovered how engineering
innovation can contribute to solving urgent environmental challenges.

Project study

Youth Scientific Research Program

From March 22nd, 2025, I participated in a seven-week Youth Scientific
Research Program focused on Toxicology and Drug Metabolism. This project
provided me with an in-depth understanding of how chemical structure
determines biological function and pharmacological effects, bridging my
knowledge of chemistry with human physiology.

Throughout the course, we explored the molecular structures and reaction
mechanisms of various drugs, studying how they interact with biological
membranes and cellular systems. Topics such as how aspirin reduces fever
and why colchicine can be toxic helped me understand how small
structural changes in molecules can lead to vastly different biological
outcomes. These discussions deepened my appreciation for the chemical
principles underlying drug design—how properties like polarity,
solubility, and functional groups affect a compound’s activity and
metabolism.

A highlight of the program was our group research paper, titled “The
Influence Mechanism and Treatment Methods of Glutathione Level in
Reducing ASD.” In this project, I was responsible for the drug chemistry
mechanisms and clinical data analysis sections. I led my team in
developing the research topic and paper structure, assigning tasks
according to each member’s strengths, and ensuring the overall coherence
of our work. This experience improved my scientific communication and
leadership skills, as I learned to integrate diverse perspectives into a
unified research direction.

Through my part of the project, I gained experience in interpreting
chemical structures from academic literature, analyzing how molecular
properties relate to biological activity. I also strengthened my ability
to handle quantitative data, using statistical tools to evaluate
experimental and clinical findings. Working with real biomedical data
showed me how chemistry, biology, and medicine converge in modern
research, and how critical accurate data interpretation is for drawing
reliable conclusions.

Beyond technical skills, this project also transformed the way I think
about chemistry. I came to see it not only as a study of substances and
reactions but also as a key to understanding life processes at the
molecular level. Drug metabolism, for example, illustrates how chemical
reactivity directly influences health outcomes—a connection that
fascinated me and inspired me to continue exploring biochemical
research.

Overall, the Youth Scientific Research Program was a milestone in my
academic growth. It deepened my understanding of applied chemistry in
medicine, taught me how to conduct and present research systematically,
and allowed me to develop as both a scientist and a collaborator. The
project’s interdisciplinary nature—linking chemistry, pharmacology, and
data science—showed me how integrated thinking can lead to innovation.
More importantly, it reinforced my motivation to pursue future studies
in biochemical research and drug development, where chemistry serves not
just as a subject of study, but as a tool for improving human life.

Conference

NK24 Model United Nations

From February 3rd to 7th, 2024, I participated in the Security Council
of NK24 Model United Nations, representing Israel in discussions
centered on the Israel–Palestine conflict. This experience was both
intellectually challenging and personally transformative. It not only
deepened my understanding of international relations but also allowed me
to strengthen my communication, critical thinking, and diplomatic
skills.

Representing Israel forced me to step outside my own perspective and
view global issues from a position that is often controversial and
complex. At first, it was difficult to justify certain policies or
defend Israel’s actions in ongoing conflicts. However, through
researching Israel’s historical background, security concerns, and
political motivations, I began to understand how each decision is shaped
by national interests and the pursuit of survival in a volatile region.
This role helped me recognize that in international affairs, there is
rarely absolute right or wrong—only competing priorities and
perspectives. It also made me realize that war, while tragic, often
persists because of the strategic benefits and political calculations
behind it. Understanding this uncomfortable truth was one of the most
valuable lessons I took away from the conference.

Moreover, participating in debates and negotiations significantly
improved my English speaking and debating skills. The sessions were
intense and fast-paced, requiring quick thinking, precise articulation,
and persuasive argumentation. I learned how to construct logical
arguments under pressure, respond confidently to counterpoints, and use
formal diplomatic language effectively. Engaging with delegates from
diverse backgrounds also exposed me to different rhetorical styles and
negotiation techniques, which helped me refine my own communication
approach.

Equally important was the experience of collaboration. While each
delegate represented a nation with its own stance, meaningful
resolutions could only emerge through compromise and cooperation. I
learned how to engage in constructive dialogue—listening to opposing
views, finding common ground, and building alliances that could lead to
consensus. Working with delegates whose positions differed sharply from
mine taught me patience, flexibility, and empathy. It showed me that
diplomacy is not about overpowering others with arguments, but about
balancing interests and fostering mutual understanding.

Overall, NK24 MUN was far more than a simulation—it was a microcosm of
real-world international politics. Through this experience, I gained a
clearer picture of how global conflicts are shaped, debated, and
sometimes resolved. I left the conference not only with stronger
communication skills but also with a more nuanced worldview and a deeper
appreciation for the complexities of peace and diplomacy. This
experience has inspired me to continue exploring international
relations, and to approach future challenges with both rational analysis
and an open mind.

Oxford Model United Nations Conference

From April 4th to 6th, 2024, I participated in OXMUN, the Oxford Model
United Nations conference held in Shanghai. I represented India in the UN Women committee, where our discussions centered on violence against women in workplaces and public spaces. Although I did not receive an award, this experience was deeply meaningful and provided me with valuable insights into international dialogue, gender issues, and personal growth.

One of the first things I noticed at the conference was the diversity and sophistication of the delegates. Many participants came from international schools in Shanghai, and compared to them, I immediately felt the difference in background and exposure. They spoke English fluently and naturally, were well-versed in international affairs, and had access to global learning opportunities such as exchange programsand political science electives. As a student from a second-tier city, I initially felt self-conscious about my comparatively limited experience and less polished communication.

However, this feeling gradually transformed into self-awareness and confidence. Through conversations with other delegates, I realized that while I might lack certain advantages in language or global exposure, I possessed other strengths that were equally important—creativity, practical reasoning, and sensitivity to local realities. These qualities became especially valuable during our debates, where we discussed how to combat gender-based violence in developing regions.

Drawing on my understanding of social and economic disparities, I proposed an initiative to establish women-centered companies in underdeveloped areas, built upon local industries and resources. Such companies would serve two key purposes: first, to help women achieve economic independence by providing stable employment, and second, to educate women about their rights and self-protection. This approach focused not just on external aid but on creating sustainable, community-driven change. My proposal sparked enthusiastic discussion among delegates and was eventually incorporated into the final resolution—a moment that filled me with pride and reaffirmed the value of thinking from multiple perspectives.

Beyond policy discussions, OXMUN also taught me how to find my place in a highly competitive environment. I learned that diplomacy is not only about eloquence or confidence, but also about observation, empathy, and the courage to share ideas that matter. Instead of comparing myself to others, I began to appreciate what I could contribute—a grounded understanding of feasibility and innovation rooted in real-world context.

In the end, OXMUN 2024 became an experience of self-discovery. It broadened my global awareness, strengthened my communication skills, and encouraged me to view challenges as opportunities to learn rather than limitations. More importantly, it reminded me that meaningful participation in global dialogue comes not from where one begins, but from how one thinks, learns, and acts to make a difference.

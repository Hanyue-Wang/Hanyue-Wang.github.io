Project study

collaborative museum studies program

I participated in a collaborative museum studies program jointly
organized by the Nanjing Foreign Language School and the Da Bao’en
Temple Site Museum. The program integrated cultural heritage studies
with material science and chemistry, offering an in-depth exploration of
how modern scientific methods can be applied to the preservation,
analysis, and restoration of ancient artifacts and architectural
remains. It was divided into three main components: on-site
archaeological fieldwork, curatorial and materials study, and an
independent research project.

During the fieldwork module, I visited the Zhang Zhao Tomb and the ruins
of the ancient Changgan City. Through these visits, I was introduced to
various scientific techniques used in archaeological investigation, such
as radiocarbon dating (C-14 analysis) for chronological determination
and spectroscopic methods (including infrared and UV–visible
spectroscopy) for analyzing material composition. I was particularly
fascinated by how chemistry provides a bridge between the tangible
relics of the past and our scientific understanding of time and process.
For example, learning about the role of isotopic decay in carbon dating
gave me a new appreciation of how fundamental chemical
principles—half-life, isotopic ratios, and radioactive equilibrium—can
literally “measure time” in the archaeological record.

The second part of the program focused on the Da Bao’en Temple Site
Museum itself, where we studied both the artifacts and the unique
architectural techniques employed in the temple’s reconstruction and
preservation. We explored the ancient Chinese art of glazed tile and
colored glass production, investigating how materials science explains
the distinctive properties of these historical materials. From a
chemical standpoint, I learned that ancient liuli (glazed glass) differs
from modern glass primarily in composition and microstructure. Ancient
liuli typically contains high lead oxide and low silica content, which
enhances its refractive index and color brilliance but also increases
its fragility and susceptibility to corrosion.

We also analyzed the chemical processes involved in the production of
colored glazes, such as the use of metal oxides (e.g., Cu²⁺ for
green-blue, Fe³⁺ for amber tones, and Co²⁺ for deep blue). Understanding
the redox reactions that influence color formation helped me see how
chemistry not only enables the creation of beauty but also governs its
preservation. Moreover, by studying the differences between glass and
liuli in terms of ionic mobility, crystallinity, and resistance to
weathering, I gained a better sense of why some ancient artifacts retain
their brilliance over millennia while others deteriorate rapidly.

In the laboratory component, we learned about the chemical conservation
and restoration of artifacts. I had the opportunity to synthesize and
decompose basic copper carbonate (Cu₂(OH)₂CO₃), a key compound in
patinas and a product of copper corrosion, to understand both
degradation mechanisms and potential restoration approaches. By
comparing synthetic and naturally weathered samples under controlled
conditions, I could observe how pH, humidity, and temperature influence
the stability of carbonate layers. These experiments deepened my
understanding of how chemical equilibrium and solubility product (Ksp)
play critical roles in the long-term preservation of metal-based
artifacts.

The final stage of the program was a self-directed research project,
where I chose to investigate the application of glass–ceramic composite
materials in the restoration of liuli architectural elements. My
research aimed to explore how modern material synthesis techniques could
address the limitations of traditional liuli, such as fragility and poor
weather resistance, while preserving its aesthetic and historical
authenticity. I examined the controlled crystallization process in
glass–ceramics, focusing on the role of nucleating agents (like TiO₂ and
ZrO₂) in promoting uniform crystal growth within a glass matrix. This
balance between amorphous and crystalline phases offers improved
mechanical strength and durability—properties essential for outdoor
architectural restoration.

To support this study, I reviewed both historical literature and recent
research on solid-state reactions, ion diffusion, and
microstructure-property relationships in glass–ceramic systems. I also
compared thermal and optical properties of different silicate-based
composites, analyzing how factors like melting temperature, refractive
index, and expansion coefficient could be optimized to achieve
compatibility with ancient liuli. This independent project not only
enhanced my ability to connect chemical principles to real-world
cultural heritage applications but also trained me in basic academic
research methods—structuring a hypothesis, reviewing literature, and
organizing technical information into a coherent scientific argument.

Beyond the scientific knowledge, the program gave me a profound sense of
the interdisciplinary power of chemistry. It demonstrated how molecular
understanding can inform fields as diverse as art history, archaeology,
and architecture. Standing in front of centuries-old relics, I realized
that preserving them is not only an act of cultural respect but also a
chemical challenge—a test of how well we understand materials,
reactions, and the passage of time.

Overall, this museum program helped me integrate my interests in
chemistry and cultural heritage. It strengthened my scientific
foundation in material chemistry while cultivating a more humanistic
perspective on science as a means of preservation rather than
exploitation. The experience convinced me that the future of heritage
conservation lies in collaboration between scientists and historians,
and it inspired me to continue exploring how chemical innovation can
protect and revitalize the legacy of the past.
